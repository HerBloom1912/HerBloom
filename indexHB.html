The Global Crisis

The barriers facing young women are universal

Whether in Mumbai or Manchester, the challenges are different in form but identical in root. The world has not yet made equal space for every young woman to lead, create, and thrive. HerBloom exists to change that.

🧠
Mental Health Crisis
Anxiety, depression, and burnout are rising sharply. Teenage girls report higher rates than boys globally, driven by social media pressure and the impossible standard of perfection.
Core issue: Constant comparison
📱
Social Media & Identity
Unrealistic beauty standards, validation through likes, and cyberbullying are replacing self-worth with external approval. Girls are more affected by appearance-based comparison than any other group.
Core issue: External validation
🎓
Education vs Opportunity Gap
India has ~18 million girls out of school (UDISE+ 2024-25). The real crisis is at the Higher Secondary level — 22.8% of 16–17 year olds are not enrolled. The Class 8→9 transition is a critical dropout point, with 13.4% not progressing due to safety and infrastructure barriers.
Core issue: Enrolment ≠ completion
💰
Economic Dependence
Lack of financial literacy and limited income opportunities keep millions of women dependent. Economic dependence directly reduces decision-making power and life choices.
Core issue: Dependence = powerlessness
⚖️
Safety & Social Restrictions
Safety concerns in public spaces and online harassment, combined with cultural limitations in many societies, mean freedom of movement and opportunity is not equally distributed.
Core issue: Freedom is not equal
🌱
Loss of Purpose
Many young women feel directionless — achieving academically but disconnected from meaning. The pressure to balance career, family, and image creates identity confusion at a critical age.
Core issue: Achievement without purpose

What We Do

One flagship mission. Two powerful pillars.

🎯 Flagship Issue
Girls' Education-to-Opportunity Pathway in India
We focus on the critical gap between a girl being in school and a girl having real opportunity. Our programmes bridge education, digital skills, confidence, and economic access — turning potential into power.
💻
A
Digital Access & Digital Confidence
South Asia has one of the world's largest gender digital divides. Girls are 35% less likely to have digital skills. HerBloom delivers digital literacy, safe online navigation, and the confidence to be creators — not just consumers — of technology. We also address digital wellbeing for Western audiences, where WHO data shows girls face rising mental health risks from social media.
👑
B
Leadership, Life Skills & Micro-Support
OECD research shows boys are twice as likely as girls to aspire to leadership by age 15. HerBloom runs youth leadership labs, public speaking workshops, mentorship programmes, and confidence circles — in India and through Western school chapters. We also provide micro-grants and targeted financial support, because sometimes the most powerful thing we can do is remove one specific barrier.

The Founder

A 16-year-old building something the world is watching

HerBloom was born not from a boardroom, but from a classroom. Rumaisha Alam Khan — a 16-year-old IB student in Ooty, India — looked at the world's data on girls and decided that understanding a problem was not enough. Something had to be done.

Rooted in her study of microeconomics, Rumaisha built HerBloom on a simple but powerful insight: individual actions, when combined, transform systems. That is not idealism — it is economics applied to social change.

A youth-led initiative founded by a teenager is distinctive, media-friendly, and emotionally compelling. But HerBloom is more than a story. It is a credible, strategic, and scalable model for girls' empowerment — built by someone who refuses to wait until she is older to begin.

"I did not wait for permission to begin. Neither should any girl."
